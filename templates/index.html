Tweet the People

Tracking Twitter sentiment about the 2020 US Presidential Election candidates.

About this project

In 2020, like in 2016, the polls vastly underestimated the electoral success of Donald Trump. There’s a lot of noise in polling: Voter suppression means everyone isn't equally able to have their vote counted, and you might be weighting data incorrectly. This project uses computational linguistics methods on Twitter data to gauge voters' intentions outside of a polling context.

Data collection

This corpus contains over 300k tweets collected between 30 September and 8 November 2020. I sampled tweets about the four presidential candidates, streaming tweets 5 times roughly a minute per 2 hours for each candidate (this was done to circumvent Twitter’s rate limits). I excluded tweets that contained no text. To see the code, look at my GitHub repository.

Sentiment analysis

I used VADER, a lexical sentiment analysis tool. This is a prototype, and I’m planning to expand the sentiment analysis using more sophisticated tools. One limitation is that VADER assigns positive scores to the word “positive” — that’s at odds with the presumed sentiment of a phrase like “tested positive for coronavirus.” I'll address this in future versions.

Geolocation

Twitter users can choose to geotag their tweets, but only about 1-2% of tweets are geotagged. Most locations in this data set are determined based on users’ self-reported location. Some people list their location as “God Bless America” or “hell since 2016”, but most locations are at least plausible. Since no-one has any clear incentive to lie about their location, I’ll assume that these are mostly truthful — but should be taken with a grain of salt.

Limitations of this project

Twitter, unlike the overall US electorate, is 80% millennial, 70% male, and skews educated, well-off, and left-leaning. But the point of the project is to understand the kind of ambient “chatter” that might indirectly influence voters’ choices. That gets picked up by Twitter users — giving us a sense of the topics a broader group of voters care about.
